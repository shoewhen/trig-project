A Vindication on Tau

Shaine Wei

Introduction

The Tau vs. Pi debate is intense within the mathematics realm, and I’ve chosen to contribute my own opinions and thoughts to this heated controversy, just because I totally chose to do this on my own and didn’t have a math teacher assign this as a project. To quickly summarize what Pi and Tau are, here's the definition I've known them by:

Pi is the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its diameter, represented by the greek letter \(\pi\)

Tau is the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its radius, represented by the greek letter \(\tau\). Tau can be thought of as "two \(\pi\)."

\(\tau\) is equal to “two \(\pi\)” since the radius of a circle is half its diameter, so in order to compensate for this difference, \(\tau\) is twice as big as \(\pi\). For example, \(\pi\) is equivalent to 180°, so \(\frac{\tau}{2}\) is also equivalent to 180°. This means that \(\tau\) itself is equivalent to 360°, also known as one whole revolution around a circle. There are many applications of using \(\tau\), and some may even say it even redefines trigonometry as a whole. This webpage explores the applications of \(\tau\) by answering some simple trigonometry questions using this circle coefficient. In addition, I’ve also included math animations (Manim) which I programmed using Python to accompany each question in order to help visualize my thought process while answering these problems.

The Tau Unit Circle

There isn't anything super special about this. It's just a unit circle but instead of using \(\pi\) I just converted every angle to \(\tau\). This can be done pretty easily by just dividing each angle of a \(\pi\) unit circle by two and then changing the \(\pi\) sign to a \(\tau\) sign.

The rest of this section will use the tau unit circle I created above to find the exact values of sine and cosine for the following angles:

\(\frac{\tau}{8}\)

\(\frac{\tau}{6}\)

\(\frac{\tau}{4}\)

\(\frac{\tau}{3}\)

\(\frac{\tau}{2}\)

\(\frac{7\tau}{8}\)

\(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{8}\right)\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{8}\right)\)

\(\tau \over8\) is also equivalent to \(\pi \over 4\) or 45°. Using the unit circle, you can easily find the values of \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{8}\right)\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{8}\right)\). Since cosine and sine can be redefined as the x and y distance from the origin to a point on the unit circle, you just need to find the terminal point when you rotate \(\tau \over8\) radians counterclockwise around the unit circle. The x-coordinate of the terminal point will be the value of \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{8}\right)\), and the y-coordinate will be the value of \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{8}\right)\). I’ve created an animation below to help visualize what this would look like on the unit circle.

As the animation shows, \(\tau\over8\) is within the first quadrant of the coordinate plane, so all values of sine and cosine will always be positive. The terminal point which corresponds to this angle is \(\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\).

\(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{6}\right)\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{6}\right)\)

\(\frac{\tau}{6}\) is also equivalent to \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) or 60°. This angle lies within the first quadrant, where sine and cosine are always positive. The terminal point which corresponds to this angle is \(\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \right)\). Thus, \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{6}\right)=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{6}\right)=\frac{1}{2}\).

\(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{4}\right)\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{4}\right)\)

\(\frac{\tau}{4}\) is also equivalent to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\), or 90°. This angle lies on the x-axis and within the upper two quadrants of the coordinate plane. Since this angle lies on the x-axis, the terminal point of this angle corresponds to \(\left(0, 1 \right)\), as there is a negligible x-distance. Additionally, this angle also lies within the upper two quadrants of the coordinate plane, so the y-coordinate of the terminal point is at its greatest value of 1. This means that \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{4}\right)=1\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{4}\right)=0\).

\(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{3}\right)\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{3}\right)\)

\(\frac{\tau}{3}\) is also equivalent to \(\frac{2\pi}{3}\), or 120°. This angle lies within the second quadrant, where all the trigonometric functions are negative except for sine (cosecant is positive as well). The terminal point which corresponds to this angle is \(\left(-\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \right)\). Hence, \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{3}\right)=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{3}\right)=-\frac{1}{2}\).

\(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{2}\right)\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{2}\right)\)

\(\frac{\tau}{2}\) is also equivalent to π, or 180°. This angle lies on the y-axis and between the second and third quadrants. Since this angle lies on the y-axis, the terminal point of this angle corresponds to \(\left(-1, 0 \right)\), as there is a negligible y-distance. Additionally, this angle also lies between the two left quadrants of the coordinate plane, so the x-coordinate of the terminal point is at its smallest value of -1. Therefore, \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{2}\right)=0\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{2}\right)=-1\).

\(\sin\left(\frac{7\tau}{8}\right)\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{7\tau}{8}\right)\)

\(\frac{7\tau}{8}\) is also equivalent to \(\frac{7\pi}{4}\), or 315°. This angle lies within the fourth quadrant, where all the trigonometric functions are negative except for cosine (secant is positive as well). The terminal point which corresponds to this angle is \(\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \right)\). Thus, \(\sin\left(\frac{7\tau}{8}\right)=-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{7\tau}{8}\right)=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\).

Finding Sine and Cosine of Angles with Given Cosine Values

The question begins by stating "If \(\cos\left(\alpha\right)=\frac{5}{13}\), find the sine and cosine of the following angles:"

\(\theta=\frac{\tau}{2}+\alpha\)

\(\theta=\tau-\alpha\)

\(\theta=\frac{3\tau}{2}-\alpha\)

\(\theta=\frac{\tau}{4}+\alpha\)

To begin this question, I first created a right triangle out of the given information. I find it easier to go about this question by visualizing what \(\cos\alpha\) might look like. As I’m already aware that \(\cos{\theta}=\frac{adjacent}{hypotenuse}\), and \(\sin{\theta}=\frac{opposite}{hypotenuse}\), I’m able to create a triangle out of this information. In order to find the missing side of the triangle, I can utilize the Pythagorean Theorem. I’ve created an animation to help explain my thought process.

\(\theta=\frac{\tau}{2}+\alpha\)

Theta is in the third quadrant of the cartesian plane, where all trigonometry functions are negative except for tangent (and cotangent). This means that \(\sin\left(\theta\right)\) and \(\sin\left(\theta\right)\) will be negative. Referencing the animation, the angle alpha has been preserved in the reference angle of theta, so visually, the shape of our original triangle has not changed. However, since our triangle is now in the third quadrant, this means that both the horizontal and vertical lengths of the triangle are now negative. This results in cosine \(\left(\frac{adjacent}{hypotenuse}\right)\) and sine \(\left(\frac{opposite}{hypotenuse}\right)\) to be negative as well. Therefore, \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{2}+\alpha\right)=-\frac{12}{13}\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{2}+\alpha\right)=-\frac{5}{13}\)

\(\theta=\tau-\alpha\)

\(\tau-\alpha\) means that there is one full rotation of a circle, and then we are turning back \(\alpha\) radians. The reference angle stays the same, once again preserving the original shape of our triangle. Additionally, \(\theta\) is now in the fourth quadrant of the cartesian plane. This means that all trigonometry functions are negative except for cosine (and secant). As we already know that sine and cosine are equal to the ratio of the opposite and the adjacent side lengths of a right triangle, respectively, we can determine that \(\cos\left(\tau-\alpha\right)=\frac{5}{13}\) and \(\sin\left(\tau-\alpha\right)=-\frac{12}{13}\).

\(\theta=\frac{3\tau}{2}-\alpha\)

\(\frac{3\tau}{2}\) is equivalent to one and a half rotations around a circle, which also lands us on the same spot as a rotation of \(\frac{\tau}{2}\) radians. Once again, we are subtracting \(\alpha\) from this angle, therefore rotating backwards (clockwise) \(\alpha\) radians. Our reference angle has stayed the same (\(\alpha\)), therefore the shape of our triangle has also been preserved within this circle. Now our triangle is in the second quadrant, where all trigonometry functions are negative except for sine (and cosecant). This means that cosine will be negative. We are resulted with \(\sin\left(\frac{3\tau}{2}-\alpha\right)=\frac{12}{13}\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{3\tau}{2}-\alpha\right)=-\frac{5}{13}\).

\(\theta=\frac{\tau}{4}+\alpha\)

To locate \(\theta\) on the circle, we can first rotate \(\frac{\tau}{2}\) radians, and then add \(\alpha\) radians to this angle. However, this leaves us with a reference angle of \(\frac{\tau}{4}+\alpha\). The shape of our triangle has been somewhat preserved, except that the longer leg is now the horizontal leg and the shorter leg is now the vertical leg. Once again, our angle is in the second quadrant of the cartesian plane, meaning that all trigonometry functions are negative except for sine (and cosecant). Additionally, since the lengths of our original triangle have been switched in our current triangle, so have the values of sine and cosine. The animation shows that \(\sin\left(\frac{\tau}{4}+\alpha\right)=\frac{5}{13}\) and \(\cos\left(\frac{\tau}{4}+\alpha\right)=-\frac{12}{13}\).

Rotations

This section answers the question “Find all the angles that satisfy the equations given below:”

\(\sin\left(\theta\right)=\frac{1}{2}\)

\(\cos\left(\theta\right)=-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\)

\(\sin\left(\theta\right)=\frac{1}{2}\)

If the sine of an angle is positive, then we already know that this angle lies in both the first and second quadrant of a cartesian plane. If we first restrict the value of theta to between 0 and \(\tau\), then we can determine that there are only two angles that satisfy this equation: one in the first quadrant, and one in the second quadrant. Using the tau unit circle, we can determine the two angles where \(\sin\left(\theta\right)=\frac{1}{2}\). These two angles are \(\frac{\tau}{12}\) and \(\frac{5\tau}{12}\). In the animation below, you can see that when \(\sin\left(\theta\right)=\frac{1}{2}\), the vertical distance of the point on the unit circle from the x-axis is \(\frac{1}{2}\). This is one of the definitions of \(\sin\left(\theta\right)\): the vertical distance from a point on the unit circle to the x-axis.

Now what if we unrestrict the value of \(\theta\)? This gives us infinite solutions of \(\theta\). This is because you can infinitely add on more full rotations around the unit circle, and the angle will always end up on the same spot in the unit circle. We already know that \(\frac{5\tau}{12}\) is one solution to this equation, so we can indefinitely add \(\tau\) to \(\frac{5\tau}{12}\) and always end up in the same position in the unit circle. The same goes for \(\frac{\tau}{12}\)! The equation looks like this:

\(\theta=\frac{5\tau}{12}+k\cdot\tau\)

\(\theta=\frac{\tau}{12}+k\cdot\tau\)

Make note that \(k\) is a whole number constant, otherwise we wouldn’t be adding full rotations to our original angle.

\(\cos\left(\theta\right)=-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\)

The cosine of \(\theta\) is negative, indicating that \(\theta\) lies in both the second and third quadrants of the cartesian plane. Using the same method we used to solve for theta in the previous question, we can determine that there are only two angles that satisfy this equation: one in the second quadrant and one in the third quadrant. The tau unit circle indicates that \(\theta\) is equal to \(\frac{5\tau}{12}\) and \(\frac{7\tau}{12}\). Just like how \(\sin\left(\theta\right)\) is the vertical distance from a point on the unit circle to the x-axis, \(\cos\left(\theta\right)\) is the horizontal distance from a point on the unit circle to the y-axis. Unrestricting the value of \(\theta\) will give us infinite solutions of \(\theta\). The equation looks like this:

\(\theta=\frac{5\tau}{12}+k\cdot\tau\)

\(\theta=\frac{7\tau}{12}+k\cdot\tau\)

Graphing Trig. Functions Using Tau

In this section, I graphed the following trigonometric functions using τ in place of π. Just like all previous animations, I coded some Manim animations in Python to visualize these graphs.

\(y=3\sin\left(x\right)\)

\(y=\sin\left(3x\right)\)

\(y=-2\cos\left(x\right)\)

\(y=\cos\left(x-\frac{\tau}{4}\right)\)

\(y=\sin\left(2x-\frac{\tau}{2}\right)\)

\(y=\cos\left(3x-\tau\right)+4\)

\(y=3\sin\left(x\right)\)

The graph of \(y=3\sin\left(x\right)\) is a vertical dilation of the function \(f\left(x\right)=\sin\left(x\right)\) by a factor of 3. \(y=3\sin\left(x\right)\) has a period of \(\tau\), amplitude of 3, and a midline at \(y=0\) with no phase shift. The animation below shows how \(y=3\sin\left(x\right)\) is transformed from \(f\left(x\right)\).

\(y=\sin\left(3x\right)\)

The graph of \(y=\sin\left(3x\right)\) is a horizontal dilation of the function \(f\left(x\right)=\sin\left(x\right)\) by a factor of \(\frac{1}{3}\). This function has a period of \(\frac{tau}{3}\), amplitude of 1, and a midline at \(y=0\) with no phase shift. The animation below shows how \(y=\sin\left(3x\right)\) is transformed from \(f\left(x\right)\). Interestingly, we find that there are three full cycles of the function within one \(\tau\). This makes a lot more sense than saying that the period is \(\frac{2\pi}{3}\), meaing there are three full cycles within \(2\pi\). Using \(\tau\) in this instance makes graphing trigonometric functions much easier and cleaner.

\(y=-2\cos\left(x\right)\)

The graph of \(y=-2\cos\left(x\right)\) is a vertical dilation of the function \(f\left(x\right)=\cos\left(x\right)\), as well as a reflection over the x-axis. This function has a period of \(\tau\), amplitude of 2, and a midline at \(y=0\) with no phase shift. The animation below shows how \(y=-2\cos\left(x\right)\) is transformed from \(f\left(x\right)\).

\(y=\cos\left(x-\frac{\tau}{4}\right)\)

The graph of \(y=\cos\left(x-\frac{\tau}{4}\right)\) is a horizontal shift of the function \(f\left(x\right)=\cos\left(x\right)\). The function has a period of \(\tau\), an amplitude of 1, and a midline at \(y=0\), with a phase shift of \(\frac{\tau}{4}\) units. The animation below shows how \(y=\cos\left(x-\frac{\tau}{4}\right)\) is transformed from \(f\left(x\right)\). After the transformation, we find that the graph of \(y=\cos\left(x-\frac{\tau}{4}\right)\) is equivalent to the graph of \(y=\sin\left(x\right)\). This is because sine and cosine are complementary functions, and always off by \(\frac{\pi}{2}\), or \(\frac{\tau}{4}\). By shifting \(f\left(x\right)\) \(\frac{\tau}{4}\) units to the right, the graph maps onto the graph of \(y=\sin\left(x\right)\).

\(y=\sin\left(2x-\frac{\tau}{2}\right)\)

The graph of \(y=\sin\left(2x-\frac{\tau}{2}\right)\) is a horizontal shift and dilation of the function \(f\left(x\right)=\sin\left(x\right)\). The function has a period of \(\frac{\tau}{2}\), an amplitude of 1, a midline at \(y=0\), and a phase shift of \(\frac{\tau}{4}\) units. The reason why the phase shift is not \(\frac{\tau}{2}\) and is instead \(\frac{\tau}{4}\) is because the phase shift of a sine function in the form \(y=\sin\left(ax-b\right)\) is given by the formula \(\frac{b}{a}\). In the given graph, \(a=2\) and \(b=\frac{\tau}{2}\). Therefore the phase shift is \(\frac{\frac{\tau}{2}}{2}=\frac{\tau}{4}\). The animation below shows how \(y=\sin\left(2x-\frac{\tau}{2}\right)\) is a transformation of \(f\left(x\right)\).

\(y=\cos\left(3x-\tau\right)+4\)

The graph of \(y=\cos\left(3x-\tau\right)+4\) is a vertical shift, horizontal shift, and horizontal dilation of the function \(f\left(x\right)=\cos\left(x\right)\). The function has a period of \(\frac{\tau}{3}\), an amplitude of 1, a midline at \(y=4\), and a phase shift of \(\frac{\tau}{3}\) units. The animation below shows how \(y=\cos\left(3x-\tau\right)+4\) is a transformation of \(f\left(x\right)\).

After watching the animation, you may have noticed that the horizontal shift by \(\tau\) units did not change the graph. Visibly, you are correct. This is because subtracting \(\tau\) from the argument of the cosine function doesn't alter its behavior due to the periodic nature of the cosine function, which has an original period of \(\tau\).
